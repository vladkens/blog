## Open Source

This year, I didn‚Äôt release any new open-source projects, although I had some work in progress. I‚Äôm still maintaining my older projects little by little, but also without much enthusiasm (for some of them, this was the main idea from the start ‚Äî stability).

More details about the main projects:

- [apigen-ts](https://github.com/vladkens/apigen-ts) ‚Äî in the near future, the codegen will need to be rewritten using templates, because [TypeScript was rewritten in Go](https://devblogs.microsoft.com/typescript/typescript-native-port/), and there is a chance that AST primitives will be removed from the `typescript` package on npm. Also, for me personally, the project has become a bit less relevant due to using tRPC/NextJS in my main stack, but for non-JS backends it is still a must-have;
- [macmon](https://github.com/vladkens/macmon) ‚Äî it is also quite difficult to maintain, because Apple releases new processors too often and in many different configurations, so it‚Äôs not always possible to get the required hardware for testing. - [ecloop](https://github.com/vladkens/ecloop) ‚Äî I added SIMD optimizations for SHA-256 and RMD-160 to the library. I think this was my most interesting task this year. I wanted to play around with CUDA and even ordered a computer for that, but ended up not using it. As for crypto itself, I got into degen stuff (more on that later).

Public projects on GitHub ‚Äî a total of 92 commits, 79 closed issues, 11 releases created, and more than 1k stars gained.

UPD: I remembered that at the end of 2024 / beginning of 2025, I worked on [badges.ws](https://github.com/vladkens/badges) in Rust. It looked like a fairly simple task, but turned into several months of work and debugging various APIs. ## Blog

Overall, this year I wanted to write more posts, and I even have many drafts, but at some point I wanted to switch to a more convenient engine than [Zola](https://www.getzola.org/) (which I currently use). However, I couldn‚Äôt find anything good and flexible enough.

Problems with Zola:

- a strange templating language that lacks some features, while issues remain open for years;
- GPT tools don‚Äôt handle this templating system well ‚Äî every time I need to make a change, it‚Äôs hard even to google how to do it;
- lack of flexibility in page navigation and file organization (I can‚Äôt arrange blog files the way I want and have to follow the engine‚Äôs structure);
- broken redirects in content ‚Äî Zola doesn‚Äôt understand relative links in text (all links must be written relative to the site navigation), so it‚Äôs not convenient to write and work with the blog locally: linking between my own posts and being sure that all links are valid. Right now, I have to solve this with a separate script.

As an engine, I would like something that uses JSX as a templating language, has Tailwind CSS out of the box, doesn‚Äôt have Zola‚Äôs navigation and linking issues, and is a simple and stable solution. Overall, [Astro](https://astro.build/) fits this category, and I tried migrating the blog to it, but it also has a number of its own problems:

- duplicated logic and the difficulty of choosing between `.astro` and `.jsx` formats;
- lack of proper MD/MDX integration (any customization is done via CSS classes instead of properly overriding the rendering of Markdown tags with JSX components);
- no built-in Atom feed generation (only RSS is supported), which means it has to be implemented manually.

Overall, I did a test migration of the blog to Astro, but never released it, because the solution felt too complex. A blog is something you might not touch for months, and when you finally do, you want to immediately understand how things work, rather than having to google or ask GPT for solutions.

I also considered NextJS as an option, since it can be configured to fit your needs, but there are big concerns about its stability. I really don‚Äôt want to read changelogs every few months just to understand their new vision and figure out what needs to be updated in something that already worked, just to keep it working the same way.

That turned into a lot of text about the blog. In short, I‚Äôd like to write more while being less distracted by technical problems. The ideal solution for me right now would be MD/MDX with the ability to override elements. Maybe someone knows a good existing solution? (or maybe it‚Äôs an idea for a project on Bun)

## Skills

In previous years, I explored and practiced DevOps, Rust, Go, LLMs, NestJS/NextJS, and other things, but this year felt neutral. I can‚Äôt quickly name anything new that I learned this year. Probably the main change in my work environment was the [switch from iTerm2 to WezTerm](/from-iterm-to-wezterm/). The reason was adding LLM features to the terminal and a [security vulnerability](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=42579472).

Overall, throughout the year it felt scary to update software. A lot of things are now being built with AI, and the quality of releases has become frankly weaker. For example:

- I‚Äôve been using Chrome since 2009, and this year was the first time I saw rendering bugs, crashes, and similar issues;
- Google Search was broken for several months, and they didn‚Äôt seem to care (if you type a query with a typo, Search suggests the correct version, you can click it and it appears in the search field, but if you then click into the field, it reverts back to the original typo ‚Äî this might seem like a small issue to some, but it‚Äôs a very common use case for me);
- on YouTube, for several days after releasing the new player, the volume couldn‚Äôt be increased above 60% via the UI: the slider just wouldn‚Äôt move. I don‚Äôt understand how something that wasn‚Äôt even properly tested in production could be released;
- many similar small bugs that ruin the UX.

In short, something has gone wrong in the world of software development. That‚Äôs why updating feels risky ‚Äî you never know how your workflow will break this time. My favorite app is [Sublime Merge](https://www.sublimemerge.com/): updates there are purely cosmetic and happen only a couple of times a year.

## Crypto aka Degen

This area deserves a separate mention. In January, I worked on a small project for bridging ERC-20 / SPL tokens between the Ethereum and Solana networks. Around the same time, Trump released his [Meme Coin](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=42749681): those who were insiders or just fast made good money, while everyone else ended up at a loss. That got me interested in how crypto works in general ‚Äî not the technical side, but the community itself.

As a result, I subscribed to various chats and other sources with related information (though it‚Äôs a huge stream, often full of noise). Later, I found the [DoubleTop community](https://www.doubletop.io/), where I‚Äôm now a member and communicate regularly.

Overall, during the second half of the year I was moderately active in crypto ‚Äî trying different areas to understand how things work. So far, my experience is this: it is possible to make money in crypto, but it‚Äôs really difficult and requires a lot of time and attention. Losing money is much easier. There are no huge multipliers on deposits, but steady percentages are possible.

Cases I participated in:

- [Sale Fleek on CoinList](https://coinlist.co/fleek) ‚Äî rekt ‚àí70%, the team basically scammed;
- [Sale Enso on CoinList](https://coinlist.co/enso) ‚Äî good result, +70%;
- [Sale Plasma](https://crypto.news/plasma-raises-500m-in-oversubscribed-xpl-token-sale-for-stablecoin-focused-blockchain/) ‚Äî x10 on allocation, but it was hard to get in, and funds were locked for 3 months;
- Sale Pump.fun ‚Äî x2 in 3 days, but as always, there were nuances. I was lucky to get part of the allocation through the website, while CEXs issued refunds;
- [Almanak on Legion](https://app.legion.cc/leaderboard/almanak/overview) ‚Äî rekt ‚àí50%. Overall, it was a decent project, but the team delayed the launch and went live in a bad market. I forgot that I participated in this sale and didn‚Äôt sell the tokens in time;
- [Farm Paradex](https://app.paradex.trade/r/vladkens) ‚Äî x2 on spend. The project was expected to launch in summer, but the release was later postponed indefinitely. This was my first retrodrop-focused project, so my enthusiasm for farming other projects cooled down. In November‚ÄìDecember, the Paradex team added an XP transfer option, which made it possible to sell points OTC, and I used that;
- [Farm Lighter](https://app.lighter.xyz/?referral=VLADKENS) ‚Äî a lot of multiples here. I earned most of my points from hedge positions and didn‚Äôt farm aggressively on purpose. Given how bad the market is right now, the project literally gave everyone a New Year‚Äôs gift;
- Binance Alpha ‚Äî simple daily activity that required generating trading volume on Binance. It brought about $300‚Äì400 per month per account, but it was hard to multi-account;
- [Sales on BuidlPad](https://buidlpad.com/) ‚Äî the platform provided stable returns for about half a year. It was easy to multi-account, but accounts were also often blocked. A lot of liquidity was required to participate, since allocations were small. On average, all sales ended up around x2 on spend;
- [Flash crash on Oct 10](https://www.coindesk.com/markets/2025/10/12/altcoins-cratered-in-oct-10-crypto-flash-crash-as-bitcoin-held-up-wiston-capital-says) ‚Äî the whole crypto market dropped hard within a couple of hours and still hasn‚Äôt returned to previous levels. I didn‚Äôt have any perp positions, so I got lucky there, but I did have USDT/USDC loans backed by crypto assets that were liquidated;
- Spot positions (bad market): BNB +30%, ETH +20%, SOL ‚àí20%, HYPE ‚àí40%.

Of course, there were more cases ‚Äî I listed the main closed ones. In general, it is possible to make money in crypto, but it‚Äôs really hard and requires a systematic approach. The crypto market is alive only for a few months a year, when so many events happen that it‚Äôs impossible to participate in everything (not enough capital, energy, or time). For most of the year, nothing really happens ‚Äî that‚Äôs when people go work at McDonald‚Äôs.

That quiet period is usually the time to farm retrodrops, but it‚Äôs genuinely difficult work: the results of your actions and money spent come only after 6‚Äì12 months, and psychologically that‚Äôs hard for me.

The current meta is Perp DEXes (Paradex / Lighter / Aster / Extended / Pacifica.fi / Ethereal / Trade.xyz / etc.) and opinion markets (Polymarket / Opinion / Limitless / etc.).

The hardest part of crypto for me is tracking balances and understanding whether I‚Äôm up or down overall (it‚Äôs basically real accounting work). Roughly speaking, I finished the year at about the same level I started, although at some points my deposit was up to +50%. It‚Äôs important to note that everything related to crypto should be done with proper risk control ‚Äî I have a separate budget that I use only for experiments.

Anyway, this is a big topic, and I‚Äôll probably need to talk about it separately.

## Health

Still alive ‚Äî and that‚Äôs good.

This year I [quit smoking](https://x.com/uid127/status/1941981050499965175) ‚Äî I‚Äôm very happy about it, I feel better, and I‚Äôve reduced the risk of various cancers. Overall, quitting wasn‚Äôt that hard: I watched a few YouTube videos to understand what to expect and what kind of ‚Äútricks‚Äù my brain would use to convince me to smoke again. For the first three weeks or so, you constantly think about smoking, and then it fades. Most of this ‚Äúthinking‚Äù isn‚Äôt about smoking itself, but about habits and routines ‚Äî like taking a break, looking out the window, having a coffee, and going for a smoke. I also started doing regular stretching/exercises ‚Äî at least a couple of times a week (I track it in Sheets). Without this, I genuinely feel worse: it‚Äôs hard to work when your back hurts, for example. I periodically take body measurements, and probably the main change is my biceps: +2 cm.
